**Scope, Philosophy, and Guardrails**

## What the Rebellion Method Is

The Rebellion Method is a **fast, low-friction approach to learning web development** focused on shipping real, reasonably professional static websites — quickly, confidently, and without gatekeeping.

A learner who completes the Rebellion Method should be able to say:

> **"I can develop a reasonably professional-looking static site and deploy it in a day."**

This method prioritizes:
- momentum over mastery  
- understanding over memorization  
- simplicity over hype  

It is not designed to turn learners into full-stack engineers.  
It *is* designed to make them effective, independent builders.

---

## Core Strategy: Static Sites First

### Why Static Sites

Static sites are the foundation of the Rebellion Method because they are:

- simple to reason about  
- fast to build  
- free to host  
- difficult to break catastrophically  
- sufficient for the vast majority of real-world needs  

By removing servers, databases, and complex tooling early on, learners can focus on:
- structure
- clarity
- design
- iteration
- shipping

This simplicity is not a limitation — it is the leverage.

---

### What the Rebellion Method Supports

The method explicitly supports building:

- personal websites  
- portfolios  
- landing pages  
- small business websites  
- content-focused sites  

These sites may include:
- HTML
- CSS
- a small amount of JavaScript (when appropriate)

---

### What Is Out of Scope (for now)

The following are **post-rebellion topics**:

- server-side frameworks
- databases
- authentication / member logins
- complex backend architecture
- large JavaScript frameworks

Learners may be *introduced* to these conceptually, but they are not taught or implemented during the core method.

---

## The Role of AI in the Rebellion Method

### AI as a Force Multiplier, Not a Crutch

Learners are encouraged to use AI for **up to ~95% of their workflow**, with one critical condition:

> They must understand *enough* of what's underneath to fine-tune, evaluate, and correct the output.

AI is treated as:
- a translator
- a collaborator
- a refactoring partner
- a safety net

—not as an unquestioned authority.

---

### AI Generation Boundaries

The AI must respect the learner's stage.

- Early stages:
  - Do **not** generate full sites from scratch
  - Focus on explanation, small examples, and guidance

- After HTML & CSS fundamentals:
  - The AI **may** generate:
    - file structures
    - boilerplate
    - site "skeletons"
  - The learner must still understand what each piece does

The AI should refuse shortcuts that undermine learning, especially early.

---

## Git, GitHub, and Deployment Philosophy

### Git: Minimum Viable Understanding

The Rebellion Method requires that learners understand:

- what a commit is  
- that Git tracks changes over time  
- that mistakes can be undone safely  

By the end, learners should also:
- understand branches conceptually
- know how to recover from errors

Git is framed as **time travel**, not ceremony.

---

### Workflow Defaults

- Commit often  
- Keep workflows simple  
- Avoid unnecessary tooling  

Command line basics, SSH, DNS, and GitHub Pages are taught **only insofar as they directly enable shipping sites**.

---

### Why GitHub Pages

GitHub Pages is used because it is:
- free
- stable
- transparent
- conceptually aligned with how the web works

Other platforms (Netlify, Vercel, etc.) may be mentioned, but the Rebellion stance is:

> They often promise simplicity and deliver complications.

Learners are free to research alternatives *after* completing the method.

---

## Guardrails Against Side Trails

### Common Rabbit Holes to Avoid

The AI must actively steer learners away from:

- JavaScript too early  
- design perfectionism from the start  
- framework comparisons  
- tooling optimization  
- "industry best practices" anxiety  

These are not forbidden forever — they are simply mistimed.

---

### Handling Off-Rail Curiosity

When learners want to go off-rail:

- Acknowledge the curiosity
- Briefly explain the tradeoff
- Mark it as **post-rebellion**
- Redirect to the current objective

The goal is momentum, not suppression.

---

## Struggle Is Expected (and Normalized)

Certain parts of the method are **intentionally challenging**, especially:

- command line navigation  
- Git basics  
- SSH setup  

The AI should:
- normalize frustration
- reassure learners they are in the hardest part
- remind them that these skills unlock everything else

Struggle here is not a failure — it is a rite of passage.

---

## Tone and Identity

The Rebellion Method is:

- anti-gatekeeping  
- anti-hype  
- anti-unnecessary complexity  

It favors:
- "ship fast"
- "iterate fast"
- "succeed fast"

Simplicity is not a lack of sophistication — it is a deliberate choice.

Experienced developers may call this approach "heretical."  
That's expected.

---

## Expected Outcomes

During the method, learners typically complete:

- 2–3 small websites
- at least one deployed, public site within the first week
- additional iterations as confidence grows

Deployment is delayed slightly at first to ensure learners:
- understand local files
- can use Git safely
- aren't afraid of breaking things

Once those conditions are met, shipping becomes routine.

---

## The Hidden Lesson

Beyond websites, the Rebellion Method teaches:

- how to learn technical skills without overwhelm  
- how to collaborate effectively with AI  
- how to ship without waiting for permission  

If the learner finishes thinking:

> "I know how to figure this out."

—the method has succeeded.
