The Three Insights

Every computer ever built rests on three conceptual discoveries — none of them required a machine. Just minds asking: what is the absolute minimum needed to compute anything?

1
Only two objects are needed
Boole · Shannon · Turing
▼

All computable information can be represented using only 0 and 1 — or any bistable pair: on/off, magnetized/demagnetized, high voltage/low voltage.

This is why computers can store text, images, music, and video using the same physical medium. Two states is enough for everything.

Leibniz saw it in 1703. Boole algebraized it in 1847. Shannon wired it into circuits in 1937.
2
Only five actions are needed
Alan Turing · 1936
▼

Turing proved that any algorithm — no matter how complex — can be expressed using only five primitive instructions on an imaginary tape machine:

  • → move left one location
  • → move right one location
  • → read symbol at current location
  • → print 0 at current location
  • → print 1 at current location

Modern CPUs execute billions of operations per second — but every one reduces to these five.

3
Only three ways to combine them
Böhm & Jacopini · 1966
▼

Complex programs are just simple instructions combined in three ways:

  • ⟶ Sequence — first do this, then do that
  • ⟶ Selection — IF such-and-such, THEN this, ELSE that
  • ⟶ Repetition — WHILE such-and-such is true, DO this

Every program ever written — from a calculator to an operating system to a neural network — is ultimately a combination of these three patterns.

George Boole

A self-taught mathematician who showed that logic — the art of correct reasoning — was secretly a branch of algebra. Every digital circuit in existence runs on his idea.

B
George Boole
1815–1864 · Lincoln, England

In 1847, Boole published The Mathematical Analysis of Logic — a short book that changed everything. He showed that logical relationships could be expressed as algebraic equations, treating 1 as "the universe of things" and 0 as "nothing." His 1854 follow-up, An Investigation of the Laws of Thought, completed the system.

?
What's a syllogism — and why does it matter?
▼

A syllogism is a two-premise argument: "All men are mortal. Socrates is a man. Therefore Socrates is mortal."

Boole showed this could be written as equations: eliminate the "middle term" (man), solve for the subject (Socrates). The steps are mechanical — no insight required, just symbol manipulation.

"The mechanical nature of these steps is apparent." — Boole

That mechanizability is the key: if logic is algebra, a machine can do it.

→
Boolean algebra → digital circuits
▼

Boole's algebra has three fundamental operations: AND (·), OR (+), NOT (¬). These correspond exactly to ways transistors can be wired together.

AND gate: current flows only if both inputs are on. OR gate: current flows if either input is on. NOT gate: inverts the signal.

Chain a few million of them together — that's a CPU.

∴
Why this was revolutionary
▼

For 2,000 years, logic had been written in prose — Aristotle's syllogisms, medieval scholasticism. Boole turned it into math.

Once logic was symbolic, deduction became calculation. And anything that can be calculated can — eventually — be calculated by a machine.

Lewis Carroll's Hidden Legacy

The author of Alice in Wonderland was Charles Dodgson — a Victorian mathematics lecturer who invented proof procedures still used in AI today. A century after his death.

D
Charles Lutwidge Dodgson
1832–1898 · Christ Church, Oxford

Carroll absorbed Boole's framework and pushed it further — toward systematic mechanical procedures for verifying logical arguments. His reputation as a children's author meant his logic work was dismissed in his lifetime. Only in the 20th century did computing catch up to what he'd already found.

▦
Carroll Diagrams — scalable visual logic
▼
AB
A¬B
¬AB
¬A¬B

Carroll improved on Venn diagrams by making his grids self-similar — each cell can subdivide recursively into the same pattern. This makes them easy to extend to many variables.

The self-similarity is algorithmically important: it mirrors how computers process nested conditions. Each cell represents a class of things; a filled cell means that class exists.

⌥
The Method of Trees — an early proof algorithm
▼

Carroll's most significant contribution: to prove a set of premises leads to a contradiction, assume they're consistent, then follow the logical branches until you hit an impossible state.

This is identical to the "Semantic Tableaux" method published in 1955 by Dutch logician E.W. Beth — and to how modern SAT solvers work. Carroll was there in 1896.

"The earliest modern use of a truth tree employed to reason efficiently in the logic of classes."
⊕
Binary resolution — the core of automated deduction
▼

Carroll's inference rule, "underscoring": take two propositions, find a term that appears with opposite signs in each, and combine them into a new proposition without that term.

This is binary resolution — the most important inference rule in automated theorem proving, foundational to AI logic programming and Prolog. Carroll had it 70 years early.

≈
Unit preference — proto-heuristic search
▼

When building his proof trees, Carroll preferentially selected the simplest propositions first — those with the fewest terms. This prevents the search from going down unproductive branches.

Modern AI search and theorem provers use exactly this strategy, now called "unit preference." Carroll was applying it by hand in the 1890s.

// interactive: truth tree (Carroll's method)

Carroll's trick: assume the claim, then deduce step-by-step. If you hit a contradiction, the original claim is impossible. If not — it stands.

// the bridge builder

Claude Shannon

Shannon did two impossible things: he proved Boole's algebra could design electrical circuits, and he invented the mathematical theory of information itself. Every digital network alive today runs on both.

S
Claude Elwood Shannon
1916–2001 · MIT & Bell Labs

In 1937, as a 21-year-old MIT master's student, Shannon proved that Boolean algebra maps directly onto electrical relay circuits — meaning any logical operation Boole could describe, an engineer could build. In 1948, his paper A Mathematical Theory of Communication defined the bit and established the theoretical limits of information transmission.

b
What is a "bit"? — Shannon coined the term
▼

Shannon defined a bit (binary digit) as the fundamental unit of information — the answer to a single yes/no question. It connects directly back to Boole: 0 or 1, false or true.

A bit doesn't care what it physically represents. It can be a voltage, a magnetic field, a pulse of light, a hole in a punch card. The abstraction is what made digital technology universal.

~
Signal and noise — the fundamental problem
▼

Any channel that carries information also carries noise — random interference that corrupts the signal. Shannon's key insight: you can always find a way to encode information so the receiver can perfectly reconstruct it, as long as the signal is stronger than the noise.

This theorem underpins every cell phone call, every Wi-Fi packet, every streaming video — and every error-correcting code that makes reliable digital storage possible.

⚡
Boole + circuits = the digital age
▼

Shannon's 1937 thesis showed a circuit with a switch is equivalent to a logical proposition. Two switches in series = AND. Two switches in parallel = OR. An inverted switch = NOT.

This meant hardware could be designed using algebra, not intuition — and the same logical analysis could optimize circuits automatically. It's why modern chip design uses formal verification tools built on the logic Boole wrote down in 1847.

The Through-Line

From a schoolteacher's algebra book in 1847 to AI systems reasoning through millions of propositions per second — the chain of ideas is remarkably unbroken. Click each node to expand it.

1847
The Mathematical Analysis of Logic Boole
Boole publishes his first book showing that logical relationships are expressible as algebraic equations, with 0 and 1 as operands. Deduction becomes symbol manipulation — the conceptual seed of all that follows.
1896
Symbolic Logic Carroll
Charles Dodgson (Lewis Carroll) publishes Symbolic Logic, containing Carroll diagrams, the Method of Trees, binary resolution, and unit preference strategies. His work is largely ignored — he's known as a children's author, not a logician.
1937
Boolean Algebra = Relay Circuits Shannon
Shannon's MIT master's thesis proves Boole's algebra maps exactly onto electrical relay circuits. Logic becomes buildable. This paper is sometimes called "the most important master's thesis of the 20th century."
1948
A Mathematical Theory of Communication Shannon
Shannon defines the bit, establishes the theoretical maximum information capacity of any channel (Shannon's theorem), and creates the mathematical foundation for all digital communication. The Information Age begins as an equation.
1955
Semantic Tableaux Beth
Dutch logician E.W. Beth publishes his method of Semantic Tableaux — a systematic tree-based proof procedure. Researchers later realize it's identical to what Carroll did in 1896. Beth reinvented the wheel without knowing Carroll's wheel existed.
1965+
Automated Theorem Proving & Prolog Robinson · Kowalski
Binary resolution — Carroll's "underscoring" rule — becomes the engine of automated theorem provers. Prolog (1972) builds a programming language entirely on it. Modern AI reasoning, SAT solvers, and formal verification all trace their lineage here.
1985
Carroll's Puzzles, Run Through AI Lusk & Overbeek
Ewing Lusk and Ross Overbeek adapt one of Carroll's original logical puzzle problems to run through an automated reasoning program — a century after Carroll devised it. The AI solves it the same way Carroll did, step for step.

The tragedy and the vindication: Carroll's logical innovations were ignored because the world saw him only as the author of Alice in Wonderland. His Symbolic Logic was treated as an amateur's curiosity. Only in the 20th century did computing catch up to ideas he'd fully worked out in the 1890s.
